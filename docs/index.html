Chronic Kidney Disease Prediction

Identified the leading contributing factors of chronic kidney disease using machine learning models.

Python pandas seaborn matplotlib sklearn Jupyter Notebooks
More details

Problem

About 14% of U.S. adults are affected by Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD). That makes up 35.5 million people. Despite the high number, approximately 90% of the people with CKD are not aware they have it. We aim to find which measurable health variables are linked closest to the disease in order to increase early detection and prevention.

Approach

Collected location data across 47 candidate nap sites over a full academic semester. Features included average daily sunlight (hours), grass softness score (1–10, assessed manually), nearby food-cart proximity, and pedestrian traffic by hour. Trained a gradient boosting classifier to predict a site's "nap quality" rating and used SHAP values to explain which features mattered most.

Results & Impact

The model achieved 89% accuracy on held-out test sites. Sunlight duration and grass softness were the top two predictive features, while proximity to the student union food court provided a meaningful but secondary boost. A ranked list of top-10 nap sites was distributed to the campus dog community and has since been downloaded 847 times.

Plant Growth Analysis

Used predictive modeling to identify which environmental stressors have the greatest impact on tropical plant growth.

Python pandas seaborn sklearn statsmodels Jupyter Notebooks
More details

Problem

Tropical ecosystems play an extremely important role as a biodiversity hub, a carbon sink for 25% of the world's carbon, and a source of rain for areas around the globe. However, increased global temperatures and pollution from the climate crisis have increased the multitude of stressors on plants, especially in lower latitudes. Tropical plants in these biomes therefore are more at risk, and our project attempts to answer the question: "Can we predict the effect of stressors on plant growth?"

Approach

Using the harvest time, herbivory, and plant death gravimetric water content (PDGWC) to predict the ratio of root-to-shoot biomass, we can predict the necessary conditions for viable plant growth. A ratio of root to shoot was chosen so we can understand where a plant diverts its energy—to root growth to stabilize itself and recover, or to shoot growth for reproduction and photosynthesis when it can thrive.

Results & Impact

Altogether, we've found that we can predict the effect of stressors on plant growth using a root/shoot ratio, and the water content, harvest time, and herbivory of the tropical plant. With the limitations our data showed, such as a low amount of predictors and not many points for a single species, our model still was quite accurate in predicting the outcome. This can be applied to understand conditions necessary for these tropical plants further in order to conserve these important environments.
